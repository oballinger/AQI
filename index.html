Three airborne pollutants are of particular relevance in Iraq: Particulate Matter (PM2.5), Sulphur Dioxide (SO2), and Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2). Particulate Matter is the leading cause of air pollution related deaths worldwide, and the UK government considers there to be “no safe limit” for PM2.5 exposure. Iraq has the 11th highest level of PM2.5 pollution in the world. Sulphur Dioxide, which forms sulphuric acid in the presence of humidity, is the primary cause of acid rain. The oil industry in Iraq generates significant quantities of SO2 due to the extremely high sulphur content of iraqi oil, which exceeds international standards by a factor of 1000. Nitrogen Dioxide was recently ruled to be the cause of death in the case of a nine-year-old girl who had a fatal asthma attack in London. Iraq is second only to Russia in terms of gas flaring, which is one of the primary sources of atmospheric NO2.

Conflict directly contributes to air pollution. Private diesel generators make up around 30% of Iraq’s electricity supply, owing largely to the destruction of the country’s electricity distribution infrastructure. These generators produce significant amounts of air pollution, particularly in urban centers such as Baghdad.

The most straightforward way of assessing air pollution is through the Air Quality Index (AQI). Environmental Protection Agencies around the world collect and monitor air quality using a network of ground-based sensors that measure the levels of five major pollutants: sulphur dioxide, nitrogen dioxide, PM2.5, ozone, and carbon monoxide. An AQI value for each of these pollutants is calculated, along with an overall AQI measure. The spike in AQI that San Francisco experiences in September reflects the effect of the worst wildfire season in California’s history on air pollution. Widely shared images showed the sky over the Golden Gate Bridge turning a bright shade of orange, with the sun completely blotted out by smoke. The sensor in San Francisco achieves a maximum AQI reading of 147.3, on the 11th of September 2020. The AQI sensor in Baghdad records 43 days above this level, despite the fact that the sensor was turned off for over a quarter of the year. For most of the year, the level of PM2.5 pollution in Baghdad is comparable to the air quality in California during the peak of wildfire season.

Why is Baghdad’s air so polluted? Iraq’s energy infrastructure has been decimated by successive wars. As a result of daily blackouts, private diesel generators make up around 30% of Iraq’s electricity supply. These generators produce up to 60% more air pollution than gas plants, and while large power stations are typically located outside of densely populated areas, cities such as Baghdad often have hundreds of diesel generators per neighborhood. Sadr City, a district home to roughly 15% of Baghdad’s residents, has over 600 diesel generator units.
